Veterans

  • If you’re a veteran, you are eligible to receive medical care at any VA hospital or emergency room—free of charge. This includes walk-in care, urgent care, and emergency services. You do not need a disability rating or an active claim. If you served, you’re covered.
  • Many veterans don’t even realize they’re eligible for benefits. Some are automatic—you don’t have to apply. These include a free pair of glasses once a year, hearing aids, CPAP machines, and other essentials that support your quality of life.
  • The VA Disability Portal is your starting point for filing a claim, checking your status, and understanding your benefits.
  • Need help? Organizations like the Disabled American Veterans (DAV) offer free assistance from trained advocates who can walk you through every step. You can also work with accredited attorneys or VSO representatives to make sure your claim is complete, accurate, and supported.
  • If you’re struggling emotionally, please reach out. The Veterans Crisis Line is available 24/7 at 988 (then press 1). You can also text 838255 or visit VeteransCrisisLine.net. Your life matters. Your service matters. Help is always available.

Shutdown Consequences

During a government shutdown, active-duty service members do not get paid—even though they must report for duty. This includes all branches, National Guard, and deployed personnel. Civilian support staff are furloughed unless deemed essential.

In contrast, military retirees and veterans with disabilities continue receiving their benefits. These payments are funded through mandatory spending and advance appropriations, so they are not interrupted.

This pay freeze has happened repeatedly: in 2013, 2018–2019, and now again in 2025. The longest shutdown lasted 35 days under President Trump in 2019. Each time, troops worked unpaid until Congress passed a funding bill.

Legislation like the Pay Our Troops Act has been proposed to prevent this, but it remains stalled in Congress.

Health Coverage & Care

Elizabeth Cloinger holds an empty prescription bottle in front of her granddaughter in Marianna, Arkansas. She’s income-eligible for Medicaid. She always has been. But she was dropped from the program last year—disenrolled for failing to complete a verification form she didn’t understand. She hasn’t filled that prescription in two years.

She’s not alone.

In July, Congress passed H.R. 1, adding the first-ever national work requirements to Medicaid. Starting in 2026, every working-age adult (19–64) in states with expanded Medicaid must prove they’re working, volunteering, or studying at least 80 hours a month. If they don’t—or can’t—they lose coverage.

Pregnant women, people with disabilities, and caregivers of young children are exempt. But everyone else must report compliance every six months. States must verify it. And if someone misses a form, a deadline, or a technicality—they’re out.

Nationwide, this affects 18.5 million adults. Many already work. Many can’t. And many, like Elizabeth, don’t know they’ve been dropped until the pharmacy says no.

Medicaid was designed as a safety net. Now it’s a maze. And the consequences are real: missed prescriptions, delayed care, and rising uninsured rates. A recent study found that Georgia’s work requirement pilot didn’t increase employment or coverage—it decreased Medicaid enrollment by 12 percentage points.

The new law doesn’t change who qualifies by income. It changes how they stay covered. And for millions, that means navigating a system that’s no longer just about need—it’s about proof.

The Debt Ceiling

You may notice a theme in the stories I choose. I stay neutral on the politics of the day—with one exception: the budget. Not mine. Not yours. But really, both ours.

The Federal Budget is the culprit. It’s the one Congress controls to fund the way of life we’ve all grown accustomed to. The one they use to pay our debt. The one they tap to send federal aid to nearly every state—because most couldn’t survive without it.

Pick a state. Any state. Our tax dollars fund programs those states simply can’t afford on their own. In 2025, 20% of all federal revenue—the money Washington collects from taxes—goes out to aid all 50 states. I’ve done the math. That’s nearly $1 trillion a year.

But here’s the illusion: most of that money is borrowed. The federal government doesn’t have it. It borrows it. And every time Congress raises the debt ceiling, it’s giving itself permission to borrow even more. That’s what the debt ceiling is. It’s not a budget. It’s a borrowing limit.

So when you hear that Congress is debating the debt ceiling, what they’re really debating is whether to keep borrowing money they don’t have to fund programs we all rely on. And if they don’t raise it? The government shuts down. Federal workers don’t get paid. Social Security checks stop. Military bases freeze. And the economy tanks.

That’s the illusion. The American Dream isn’t funded by taxes. It’s funded by debt. And the ceiling keeps rising.

Make Noise: The Laws Still Exist—But Who’s Enforcing Them?

I remember a time when you thought twice about running a stop sign—and running a red was out of the question. Your chances of a ticket were high. Now?

Speeding. Red lights. Border stops. Unpaid tickets. Across America, the rules haven’t changed—but the enforcement has. In border zones, CBP agents can’t issue speeding tickets, and local police are stretched too thin to patrol vast stretches of highway. Judges now require specific suspicion for immigration stops, leaving entire corridors effectively unpoliced.

Traffic violations go unanswered. In Colorado, if you ignore a ticket, it’s kicked to the courts—but many citations are never enforced, and contractors or out-of-state drivers often face no follow-up at all. Even when fines are issued, payment is treated as guilt, and contesting it requires a court appearance most drivers skip.

The result? A quiet erosion of consequence. The laws remain on the books, but for many Americans, there’s no one left to pull them over. Judges are overwhelmed. Agencies are understaffed. And federal contractors—who often receive no pay and no backpay—have little incentive to chase infractions.

This isn’t lawlessness by rebellion—it’s lawlessness by vacancy. And it’s happening in plain sight.

Why Digging For Clarity

A new phenomena is taking over the country. We all see it. We pretend it's not real. But, my opinion, it's very real. Political division that is destroying relationships. It eats at your heart to think it. I would never do that you might say. Well, I did. We no longer care what other people think unless we think alike. Anything else means the other side is just stupid. I did a little reassessment and discovered that I knew a lot of "stupid" people. Not a lot. But far too many. And it has nothing to do with our crushing debt or the many wars we're throwing our tax dollars at around the world. It has nothing to do with politics at all. Love Trump. Or hate Trump. You, are stupid. Either way. These are playground politics and I was ashamed of myself. I have my opinions of how we got there that mostly involve the media, print and live. They report news, some of it very good. But what they all have in common is that they have taken a stance; chosen a side. Many of their stories are written to induce anger. And anger drives ratings. They put that hook in and the next day you're tuning in and getting another hook. Watch us, they're all screaming. At first I thought it was only the liberal media doing this. I took a closer look and guess what? They "all" do it. News equals viewership equals money. And like it or not, the news is a very big business. The "hook" is what keeps us tuning in and wanting us to come back for more. So I wrote Digging For Clarity. I wrote it keeping you in mind but mostly I wrote it for myself. Along the journey, I only watched snippets of local news. Zero national news. That is where the fishing line and hook come in the hardest. I started reading news again after a very long time off from it. I wasn't reading the news the same way though. I was hunting for stories to put on DFC that would keep me informed but not enraged or taking sides on an issue. I have a side. Of course. Everyone does. I wanted to know what the big, fat, beautiful bill was. I wanted to know the good. Who it helps. And I wanted to know who it hurts. And by how much. What good comes from hating the person that signed it into law and the Congress that gave it to him to sign. That's not education. That's emotion. My goal on this page is to erase emotion from the news. One of the favorite sayings of our time, "it is what it is". I'm not healed from this divisional cord yet, but I am making positive progress. Maybe I'll never be cured but, I am aware of my weaknesses and I'm trying to fix them. I want to be like a Marine, the best I can be. My life is a journey, like yours is. And like all journeys, there are obstacles along the way. How you deal with them is what makes you who you are. I wish you all a wonderful journey and hope this page might help you, even if just a little bit.
