The Cost of Governance — Part 1: Federal Outlays and Obligations

  • Total Federal Outlays: $7.0 trillion — includes defense, Medicare, Social Security, and all discretionary spending
  • Federal Revenues: $5.2 trillion — mostly from income taxes, payroll taxes, and corporate taxes
  • Annual Deficit: $1.9 trillion — the gap between what the government spends and what it collects
  • Debt Held by the Public: $30.1 trillion — owed to investors, banks, and foreign governments
  • Total Federal Debt: $39.0 trillion — includes intragovernmental holdings like Social Security trust funds
  • Debt Ceiling: Raised to $41.1 trillion on July 4, 2025 — a temporary fix, not a structural solution
  • Interest Payments: $1.02 trillion — now one of the largest federal expenses, rivaling Medicare and Defense

The Cost of Governance — Part 2: The Fiscal Squeeze

  • Debt vs. GDP: The U.S. now owes about 98% of what it produces in a year. GDP is the total value of everything the economy makes—goods, services, investments. When debt nearly equals that, it signals we’re stretched thin.
  • Interest Payments: The government spent $1.02 trillion just on interest this year. That’s money that doesn’t build roads, fund research, or help veterans—it just keeps the debt from growing faster.
  • Crowding Out: As interest costs rise, there’s less room for new ideas. Infrastructure, education, and emergency response all get squeezed. The budget becomes a list of obligations, not opportunities.
  • Rate Risk: Every 1% rise in interest rates adds roughly $390 billion in new cost. That’s the price of borrowing when the debt is already maxed out.
  • Political Gridlock: Lawmakers keep raising the debt ceiling but avoid the hard fixes. The ceiling isn’t a solution—it’s a ritual. And every time it’s raised, the squeeze gets tighter.

The Cost of Governance — The Price of Inaction

  • Congress: Lawmakers continue receiving full salaries due to constitutional protections—even during shutdown.
  • Military: No Pay During Shutdown All active-duty service members, National Guard, and reservists must report for duty but will not receive pay until federal funding resumes. Civilian support staff are furloughed unless deemed essential.
  • Social Security & Medicare: Benefits continue uninterrupted, funded by permanent appropriations.
  • VA Hospitals: Veterans retain access to emergency and walk-in care at VA facilities.
  • Federal Employees: Non-essential staff are furloughed without pay; essential staff must work unpaid until funding resumes.
  • Federal Contractors: Often receive no pay and no back pay—contract law doesn’t guarantee reimbursement.
  • Air Traffic Control & TSA: Stay on duty without pay; delays possible if sick-outs increase.
  • SNAP & TANF: Food assistance continues under mandatory spending, but TANF may pause if states can’t cover the gap. Extended shutdowns risk delays.
  • WIC: Emergency food aid for women and infants may run out within days. Clinics face closure if Congress doesn’t act.
  • Medicaid: Coverage may be impacted if Congress fails to reverse recent cuts—especially for low-income families.
  • Hospitals: Remain open, but uninsured patients may face delays or denials if Medicaid access stalls.
