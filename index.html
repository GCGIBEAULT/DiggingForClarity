  • Total Federal Outlays: $7.0 trillion — Covers everything from defense and healthcare to infrastructure and interest payments.
  • Total Revenues: $5.2 trillion — The government brings in less than it spends, creating a persistent gap that fuels long-term debt.

  • Debt Held by the Public: $30.1 trillion — Financed by investors, banks, and foreign governments; now nearly equal to the entire U.S. economy.

Veterans

If you’re a veteran, you are eligible to receive medical care at any VA hospital or emergency room—free of charge. This includes walk-in care, urgent care, and emergency services. You do not need a disability rating or an active claim. If you served, you’re covered.

Many veterans don’t even realize they’re eligible for benefits. Some are automatic—you don’t have to apply. These include a free pair of glasses once a year, hearing aids, and other essentials that support your quality of life. If you’ve served, you’ve earned more than you may know.

The VA Disability Portal is your starting point for filing a claim, checking your status, and understanding your benefits.

Need help? Organizations like the Disabled American Veterans (DAV) offer free assistance from trained advocates who can walk you through every step. You can also work with accredited attorneys or VSO representatives to make sure your claim is complete, accurate, and supported.

If you’re struggling emotionally, please reach out. The Veterans Crisis Line is available 24/7 at 988 (then press 1). You can also text 838255 or visit VeteransCrisisLine.net. Your life matters. Your service matters. Help is always available.

Health Coverage & Care

This is the first page in a continuing series on health care. We’re not here to echo talking points—we’re here to dig. To ask what’s working. What’s broken. And what’s being ignored. Over time, this section will grow into a resource for anyone trying to understand how care is delivered, denied, and paid for in America.

First things first: money drives everything on Earth—including health care. America will spend $5.6 trillion on health care in 2025. That includes everything—Medicare, Medicaid, private insurance, out-of-pocket costs, and more. Of that, roughly $3.5 to $4.2 trillion is paid by the insured and their insurance companies.

If coverage were completely free, that cost wouldn’t disappear—it would shift. The taxpayer would have to absorb it. That means replacing premiums and copays with higher taxes or new federal spending. The math is simple. The politics are not. We need to start asking the right questions.

The Debt Ceiling: Congress’s Annual Disguise

Published: Friday, September 19, 2025

Every year—sometimes twice—Congress raises the debt ceiling. They call it “avoiding default.” They call it “fiscal responsibility.” But let’s be honest: it’s a ritual of avoidance. A ceremonial cloak to hide the truth about how broken our budgeting process really is.

The debt ceiling isn’t a plan. It’s a patch. A temporary fix that lets lawmakers keep spending without confronting the consequences. In July 2025, Congress raised the ceiling to $41.1 trillion—a move that allowed more borrowing but did nothing to address the underlying deficit of $1.9 trillion.

And here’s the kicker: the ceiling itself is a political invention. Before 1917, Congress had to approve every loan. But during World War I, they gave Treasury a leash—as long as it didn’t exceed a set limit. That leash has been stretched so many times it’s now a noose around future generations.

Each time Congress raises the ceiling, they’re not solving the problem. They’re deferring it. They’re telling voters, “We’ll deal with this later,” while quietly adding trillions to the ledger. The interest payments alone—$1.02 trillion this year—are now one of the largest federal expenses.

And yes, they raise it more than once a year. In 2025, they reinstated the ceiling at $36.1 trillion in January, then raised it again to $41.1 trillion by July. That’s not fiscal discipline—it’s fiscal denial.

So what’s the truth Congress is hiding?

That the debt ceiling isn’t a guardrail. It’s a mirage. A political tool used to posture, delay, and distract. It gives the illusion of control while the fiscal car speeds toward a cliff.

If Congress were serious, they’d stop raising the ceiling and start rewriting the blueprint. They’d confront the mismatch between spending and revenue. They’d stop pretending that suspending the limit is anything but a way to keep the lights on without paying the electric bill.

Until then, the debt ceiling will remain what it’s always been: a disguise. And every time it’s raised, we should ask—not how high it goes, but how long we’re willing to pretend it means anything at all.

Make Noise, Not War

Thank You, Charlie

We lost a good man this week. Killed in cold blood because he didn’t support abortion. Shot dead in front of his wife and kids because he believed in Jesus Christ. Murdered because he didn’t support homosexuality. Sent to his grave at 31 years old because he advocated tight border control.

I cried when my daughter called me, crying, to tell me about Kirk’s death. Famous people die all the time. This is the first time I cried for one of them. Don’t get me wrong—I didn’t even know the guy, so it wasn’t like I was bawling like a baby. Like his wife was. Like his children were. His extended family. His friends. I saw him on TV a few times, but it was easily enough for me to appreciate what he was trying to do—for God, his family, and his country.

Thank you, Charlie. Because of your passing, I decided to take a closer look at my life. I thought it would be a good idea to dig in and see what I believed in as strongly as Charlie did. What makes me tick? Would somebody who barely knew me cry if I died? Turns out I’m a lot like Charlie. Scarily alike. Like twins alike. Did he have anything to do with that? Not one bit. Did he influence me so much that I changed how I think about any subject? Nope. I go about my life believing what I believe. If you ask me, I’ll tell you. If you don’t, you’ll never know. I don’t know how he felt about the death penalty. And unless you ask me, you’re not going to know how I feel.

Besides the things Charlie and I believed in, we also shared something else. We loved our families with every bit of our being. We loved ourselves—the good and the bad. We respected others for who they are. If every one of us were on the same page, we’d all be attending Harvard. I bet he didn’t have an ounce of hate in him. I wish I could say the same. The last twenty years have been difficult for America. We’ve taken a downturn that no one knows how to pull us out of. Charlie had an idea that he could help, and he lived and died by it.

Thank you, Charlie. And God bless you for daring to dream. And God bless your family and friends that you’ve left behind. And God bless the haters. They may need it the most.

Why I Built This Page
